# Development Methodologies

## Waterfall

The **Waterfall methodology** is a traditional project management and software development approach in which work progresses through a series of **sequential phases**. Each phase is completed before the next one begins, with little or no overlap.

A typical Waterfall process looks like this:

1. **Requirements gathering** – Define what the system or project must accomplish.
2. **Design** – Create technical and architectural plans.
3. **Implementation** – Build the product according to the design.
4. **Testing** – Verify that the product works as intended and identify defects.
5. **Deployment** – Release the product to users.
6. **Maintenance** – Fix issues and make necessary updates after release.

| Pros | Cons |
| --- | --- |
| **Clear Structure:** Waterfall follows a linear and sequential approach, which provides a clear, easy-to-understand structure with distinct phases like requirements, design, implementation, testing, and maintenance. | **Lack of Flexibility:** Waterfall doesn’t easily accommodate changes once a phase has been completed, making it difficult to adapt to new requirements or feedback after the initial planning stage. |
| **Well-documented Process:** Each phase is completed before moving on to the next, leading to comprehensive documentation at every step. This is particularly useful in regulated industries or when knowledge transfer is important. | **Late Testing:** Testing occurs only after the development phase is complete, which means bugs or issues are often discovered late in the process, making them more costly and time-consuming to fix. |
| **Easy to Manage:** Since Waterfall has a defined scope, timeline, and deliverables from the start, it’s relatively easier to manage, especially in projects with fixed requirements. | **Risk of Misalignment:** Since the customer doesn’t see the product until late in the process, there’s a risk of misalignment between the customer’s expectations and the final product, especially if requirements were misunderstood at the start. |
| **Good for Simple or Short-term Projects:** Waterfall can work well for projects with well-defined requirements that are unlikely to change, or for small, straightforward projects with clear goals. | **Longer Delivery Times:** The linear nature of Waterfall means that you need to wait until the entire project is finished before delivering any functional software, which can delay feedback and value delivery. |
